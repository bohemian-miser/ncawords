Steering a Glider by Gradient-Modulated Kernels

A cell’s kernel doesn’t have to be fixed — it can respond to the state. Here every cell senses the local gradient of a second channel B, a “repellent” field emitted by a red dot you place, and shifts its kernel’s focus away from uphill-B. The living blob flees the dot: chemotaxis implemented purely by kernel-space steering.

The mechanism is a dipole decomposition of a trained kernel K₀. We pre-weight it by the cosine and sine of the stencil angle to get two basis kernels Kₓ and Kₕ, then let each cell compose an effective kernel K₀ + a·(sₓKₓ + sₕKₕ), where s = (sₓ, sₕ) is the unit vector pointing down B’s gradient, weighted by gradient strength. The effect is a kernel whose mass leans away from the dot, so growth is sensed — and produced — on the far side, and the pattern drifts off in that direction. The physics itself (three 15×15 kernels, growth heights, μ, σ) is a trained multik run, untouched.

This is the hand-built version of what our dynwave variant learns end-to-end: there, a small network reads local state and composes the kernel from a basis per cell, per step. Here the composition rule is written by hand so you can watch it work.

Kernels and weights are read live from a public training bucket, the same source the Lenia lab gallery uses. See the research log for how this physics was trained.
